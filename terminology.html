APPENDIXTerms

A categorised glossary covering the game theory, options machinery, microstructure, and literature touchstones that the paper draws on.

A Game theory

The model is a standard infinitely repeated game with public observability. These are the relevant solution concepts.

Stage game One-shot game

The per-period game played in isolation. In BPS, the stage game has two players, three markets, and continuous strategy spaces (resource allocations summing to ≤ 1). Each stage game has its own Nash equilibrium (Corollary 1) and Pareto frontier (Proposition 2).

Nash equilibrium NE · stable best-response profile

A strategy profile in which each player's strategy is a best response to the others'. In the stage game, the symmetric NE has both intermediaries entering all three markets with allocations proportional to arbitrage value (Eq. 1.4). It is the unique non-cooperative equilibrium of the one-shot game.

Pareto efficient Pareto optimal allocation

An allocation such that no player can be made strictly better off without making another strictly worse off. The symmetric Pareto-efficient point in BPS has each intermediary entering only her natural market, with alternation in M₃. It dominates the Nash equilibrium for both players but is not itself a Nash equilibrium of the stage game.

Subgame perfect equilibrium SPE

A strategy profile that constitutes a Nash equilibrium in every subgame, including those reached only off the equilibrium path. SPE rules out non-credible threats. In repeated games, SPE is what gives trigger strategies their bite: the punishment phase must be a credible continuation play.

Trigger strategy Grim trigger · Friedman 1971

A strategy with two modes: "cooperate" while all observed past play has been cooperative, "punish" forever (or for some finite number of periods) once a deviation is observed. BPS use the simplest form — grim trigger with permanent reversion to Nash. The trigger strategy works precisely when the future loss from triggering punishment exceeds the one-period gain from deviating.

Folk theorem Fudenberg-Maskin 1986

For sufficiently patient players (high δ), any feasible and individually rational payoff vector of the stage game can be sustained as an SPE of the infinitely repeated game. The theorem is "folk" because it was widely believed before any formal proof. The BPS tacit-collusion equilibrium is a specific application.

Discount factor δ ∈ (0, 1)

The weight a player places on next period's payoff relative to today's. The SPE condition in BPS (Eq. 2.1) requires δ ≥ (A − f − f*/2)/A. In the dividend-play empirical setting, opportunities arrive weekly, so the relevant per-period δ is close to 1 and the condition binds easily.

Tacit collusion Implicit cooperation

A repeated-game equilibrium in which players sustain a cooperative outcome without explicit communication or contract. The classical industrial-organization example is price-fixing without overt collusion (Bernheim-Whinston 1990); the BPS example is mutual non-entry without overt market-allocation agreements.

Contest-success function Tullock 1980 · Skaperdas 1996

A function specifying how a contested prize is divided across players given their effort or resource commitments. BPS uses the linear contest-success function k_i / (k_1 + k_2): your share equals your share of total committed resources. Convex contest functions (where small effort advantages yield disproportionately large prize shares) would change quantitative predictions but not the qualitative structure of equilibria.

B Options & assignment

The mechanics of the dividend-play strategy and the underlying institutional details.

American call option Early-exercisable call

A call option that can be exercised by the holder at any time up to expiration, in contrast to a European call which is exercisable only at expiry. For non-dividend-paying stocks, it is never strictly optimal to exercise an American call early; for dividend-paying stocks, early exercise just before ex-dividend can be optimal when the dividend exceeds the call's remaining time value.

In-the-money ITM · S > K for a call

A call option whose intrinsic value is positive: stock price exceeds strike. Only ITM calls on dividend-paying stocks are candidates for dividend play — for OTM and ATM calls, there is no windfall to capture even if the buyer fails to exercise.

Ex-dividend date Stock-drop date

The first trading day on which the stock trades without the right to the upcoming dividend. On the ex-dividend date, the stock price drops by approximately the dividend amount D. Holders of unexercised ITM calls lose D × 100 in value per contract; the option writer captures the corresponding windfall.

Early Exercise Value EEV · per-contract windfall

The paper's measure of the per-contract value of dividend play. Roughly, EEV ≈ D − r·K·Δt − (time value of the option). When EEV is positive, exercise is optimal for the long holder; when she fails to exercise, EEV is the windfall accruing to the writer or — if dividend play occurs — to the arbitrageur.

OCC Options Clearing Corporation

The central clearing house for U.S. listed options. Critically for this paper, the OCC performs random assignment of exercise notices to short positions: when more shorts exist than exercises, the OCC randomly selects which shorts must deliver the stock and which retain the option. The 2014 same-contract netting rule is an OCC rule.

Dividend play

The specific arbitrage strategy at the heart of the paper. By simultaneously writing many short positions and buying offsetting longs, an arbitrageur shifts the OCC's random-assignment lottery toward herself, capturing most of the windfall that would otherwise accrue to the original option writer. Typically prearranged between pairs of floor traders.

Suboptimal non-exercise Battalio, Figlewski & Neal 2020

The underlying friction. A non-trivial fraction of retail and small-institutional ITM call holders fail to exercise even when it is strictly optimal — out of inattention, computational difficulty, or operational frictions (e.g., not having enough cash to take delivery of the underlying). Without this friction, there is no dividend-play opportunity.

Greek letters Delta, vega, theta

Standard option price sensitivities: delta = ∂C/∂S (sensitivity to underlying), vega = ∂C/∂σ (sensitivity to volatility), theta = ∂C/∂t (time decay). The paper uses delta and vega to show that contracts with vs. without arbitrageur entry have nearly identical risk characteristics, ruling out operational-risk explanations for non-entry.

C Microstructure

The institutional plumbing of U.S. options trading.

Market maker Designated Market Maker · DMM

A registered firm that continuously quotes both buy and sell prices in a security and stands ready to trade. In options markets, market makers often hold large gross long/short inventories from facilitating other participants' trades. These inventory positions are what make some contracts "natural" markets for them in the BPS sense.

OPRA Options Price Reporting Authority

The consolidated source of last-sale and quote information across all U.S. options exchanges. The paper uses transaction-level OPRA LiveVol data (provided by Cboe), with floor-trade flags (SLFT, MLFT) to identify dividend-play trades.

Floor trading Physical exchange floor

Trading executed by a human market participant physically located on an exchange floor, as opposed to electronic order routing. Now a small fraction of overall options volume but a large fraction of dividend-play volume because (i) exchanges cap fees for floor-executed dividend plays, and (ii) the floor enables bilateral prearranged trades at agreed terms.

PHLX Nasdaq PHLX Options Exchange

The exchange where most U.S. dividend-play trades execute. PHLX offers a daily $1,100 fee cap for floor traders engaging in the dividend strategy on a given ticker, making the marginal cost of additional contracts within a day's strategy effectively zero. The Nasdaq PHOTO data (with order classification by counterparty type) is sourced from PHLX.

BOX BOX Options Exchange

Another exchange where dividend-play activity is concentrated, also offering fee caps for floor-executed dividend strategies. Together, PHLX and BOX account for most of the volume the paper analyses.

Bid-ask spread & midpoint Effective trade cost

The spread between best buy and best sell quotes. Dividend-play trades typically execute at or near the midpoint, meaning effective trade costs are very low — the spread doesn't bind. This is one of the reasons trading costs cannot explain the no-entry pattern (Section 4.4).

D Literature

The papers the BPS contribution sits between.

Shleifer & Vishny (1997) "Limits of arbitrage"

The seminal limits-to-arbitrage paper. Argues that real-world arbitrageurs are agents managing other people's capital, subject to performance-based withdrawals. This creates a wedge between mispricings and arbitrage activity. The arbitrageurs are competitive in their model; BPS extend the framework by making them strategic.

Gromb & Vayanos (2002) Equilibrium limits to arbitrage

A general-equilibrium model of competitive arbitrageurs facing collateral constraints across segmented markets. Studies aggregate welfare consequences of arbitrage frictions. The mechanism is competitive; BPS contrast their strategic-arbitrageur approach with this benchmark.

Green & Porter (1984) Trigger strategies in oligopoly

Foundational paper formalising trigger strategies in repeated games with imperfect monitoring. Showed how cooperative outcomes can emerge from purely non-cooperative strategic considerations. BPS's tacit-collusion result is a clean application of this machinery to multi-market arbitrage.

Bernheim & Whinston (1990) Multimarket contact

Showed that firms interacting in multiple markets can sustain collusion more easily than firms in any single market. The mechanism is "pooling of slack constraints" across markets. The BPS multi-market structure is in this tradition, but with the novel twist that cooperation involves market exit (saving fixed costs) rather than price elevation.

Dutta & Madhavan (1997) NASDAQ market-maker collusion

The closest predecessor. Argued that the puzzling avoidance of odd-eighth quotes by NASDAQ market makers (Christie-Schultz 1994) could be explained as a tacit-collusion equilibrium. BPS extends the same logic to multi-market arbitrage with natural markets, with a different (entry-cost) source of welfare gain from cooperation.

Kyle (1985) Strategic informed trading

The foundational model of strategic trading by an informed monopolist (or oligopoly of informed traders) facing competitive market makers. The microstructure literature on strategic trading branches from Kyle. BPS departs from the Kyle tradition by focusing not on price impact but on multi-market entry decisions.

Siriwardane, Sunderam & Wallen (2024) Segmented arbitrage

Empirically documents that arbitrageurs specialise in narrow market segments rather than spanning across markets. BPS builds a strategic-game-theoretic explanation for this specialisation; SSW provides the broader empirical context.

E Empirical concepts

Methods and quantities specific to the empirical part of the paper.

Pecking order hypothesis

The (rejected) hypothesis that capital-constrained arbitrageurs allocate to the most profitable opportunities first. Predicts entry rate should rise with measured profitability. BPS rejects this directly: the no-entry rate is essentially flat across profitability terciles (FIG 4.2), eliminating capital constraints as the explanation for non-entry.

Propensity score matching Rosenbaum & Rubin 1983

An identification strategy that matches each "treated" observation (here: contract with floor entry) to a "control" with similar observable characteristics. BPS use this to show that contracts with and without arbitrageur entry coexist across the full propensity-score spectrum — i.e. there's no observable characteristic that systematically predicts entry. This complements the cross-tercile evidence in ruling out pecking-order explanations.

Retail-buy imbalance Natural-market proxy 1

A contract on which retail customers are net buyers. The market makers facilitating those trades end up short the contract — making the contract a "natural" market for them if it later proves to have positive EEV. BPS uses retail-buy imbalance as one of two empirical proxies for natural markets.

Market-maker sell imbalance Natural-market proxy 2

The direct measure: contracts in which market makers (rather than retail) are the dominant net sellers. Such market makers hold short inventory in these contracts and are positioned to capture the dividend-play windfall without paying any entry cost. The complementary proxy to retail-buy imbalance.

SLFT / MLFT OPRA floor-trade flags

Single-leg floor trade and multi-leg floor trade. The OPRA trade-type codes used by the authors to isolate transactions executed from the physical exchange floor, which is where the dividend-play activity occurs because of the fee caps. The other floor-trade codes used in the sample are MLCT, MSFL, SLCN, and TLFT.
