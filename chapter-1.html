CHAPTER 1The model

Two risk-neutral arbitrageurs, three markets, fixed entry costs. The whole paper rests on what happens when this small game is repeated.

§1.1Economic environment

Time is discrete and the horizon is infinite. The economy has three markets, labelled M₁, M₂, M₃. Each market has one per-period arbitrage opportunity of fixed monetary value. To keep the algebra simple, the values are A in M₁ and M₂, and A* in M₃.

There are two risk-neutral arbitrageurs (the paper calls them "intermediaries"), labelled 1 and 2. Each lives for the full infinite horizon. Each has a fixed budget of one unit of resources per period — capital, attention, traders, take your pick — to allocate across the three markets.

The key institutional feature is the natural market. Each intermediary has one market in which her per-period fixed entry cost is zero; in any other market, she pays a positive fixed entry cost only if she actually commits non-zero resources there.

  • Intermediary 1's natural market is M₁. Her entry costs are (0, f, f*) in (M₁, M₂, M₃).
  • Intermediary 2's natural market is M₂. Her entry costs are (f, 0, f*).
  • Neither is natural in M₃; both face the same positive cost f* there.

"Natural" is the model's stylised way of saying: this market maker is already there. She holds inventory in this contract from her market-making activities; she's wired into the order flow; her balance sheet already nets the relevant risk. The marginal cost of harvesting an arbitrage opportunity in her own market is, for present purposes, zero.

§1.2Payoffs and the resource constraint

Let kij denote the resources Intermediary i commits to market j. The per-period budget constraint is

ki1 + ki2 + ki3 ≤ 1,   i ∈ {1, 2}. EQ 1.1 · BUDGET CONSTRAINT

Conditional on both intermediaries entering a market, the arbitrage opportunity in market j is split between them proportionally to their market shares of committed resources. That gives Intermediary 1's per-period payoff:

π₁(k₁¹, k₁², k₁³) = A · k₁¹
──
k₁¹+k₂¹  + A · k₁²
──
k₁²+k₂²  + A* · k₁³
──
k₁³+k₂³  − f · 𝟙k₁²>0 − f* · 𝟙k₁³>0 EQ 1.2 · PER-PERIOD PAYOFF

And Intermediary 2's payoff is the mirror image (swap the 1's and 2's, and put the f in front of 𝟙k₂¹>0). Note the two structural features:

  • Pro-rata share. If only one intermediary enters a market, she captures the entire arbitrage value A or A* there. If both enter, they split it according to their committed-resource ratio.
  • Pay the cost only on entry. The indicators 𝟙k>0 mean the fixed cost f or f* is paid only if the intermediary commits any non-zero amount to the market. Stay out, pay nothing.

Each intermediary maximises the discounted infinite sum of her per-period payoffs, with common discount factor δ ∈ (0, 1):

max   ∑t=0∞  δt  πi(ki,t¹, ki,t², ki,t³) EQ 1.3 · INTERMEDIARY'S OBJECTIVE
The contest-success function

If you've seen tournament models in IO or rent-seeking models in political economy (Tullock 1980, Skaperdas 1996), the pro-rata share k₁j / (k₁j + k₂j) is the linear contest-success function. It's a clean way to model "the more capital you commit, the larger your slice." The assumption matters: with a different (e.g. convex) contest function, the equilibrium quantities would shift but the qualitative story would survive.

§1.3The one-shot Nash equilibrium

Start with the stage game. Each intermediary takes the other's resource allocation as given and chooses her own to maximise her stage payoff. Symmetry suggests looking at the symmetric Nash equilibrium. Solving Eq. 1.2 with that ansatz gives the paper's Corollary 1:

k₁¹ = k₂¹ = k₁² = k₂² = A / (2A + A*)
k₁³ = k₂³ = A* / (2A + A*) EQ 1.4 · SYMMETRIC NASH ALLOCATION

In words: both intermediaries enter all three markets. Resource shares are proportional to the size of the arbitrage opportunity in each market — bigger A or bigger A* gets more capital. The intuition is the standard best-response logic: if your opponent commits more capital to a market, you must do likewise to maintain your share of its winnings.

Each player's stage-game payoff at this Nash equilibrium is:

πNE = A + A*/2 − f − f* EQ 1.5 · NASH PAYOFF

The intermediary captures her full share of arbitrage (half of each of M₁, M₂, M₃, summing to A + A*/2), but pays both non-natural-market entry costs f and f*. The Nash equilibrium exists provided entry is profitable in each market: A/2 ≥ f and A*/2 ≥ f*, which BPS assume throughout.

§1.4The Pareto frontier

The Nash payoff in Eq. 1.5 is not Pareto-optimal for the two intermediaries. To see why, consider an alternative allocation: each intermediary enters only her own natural market and they alternate in M₃ (each entering half the time). The arbitrage values harvested are identical to the Nash allocation, but the fixed cost paid is dramatically smaller — only f*/2 in expectation per intermediary instead of f + f*.

This cooperative allocation is the symmetric Pareto-efficient point (Proposition 2):

πPE = A + A*/2 − f*/2  >  πNE = A + A*/2 − f − f* EQ 1.6 · PARETO ABOVE NASH

The gap is πPE − πNE = f + f*/2 per period — exactly the wasted fixed-cost spending in Nash. Figure 1.2 plots the Pareto frontier (BPS's Figure 1) and the two equilibria.

The shape is worth pausing on. Slanted segments arise when one intermediary gradually shifts resources into a market while still incurring the fixed cost there. Vertical jumps occur at the discrete event of exiting a market — the fixed cost vanishes at once. The symmetric Pareto point sits on the middle segment, where both intermediaries occupy only their own natural market and randomise entry in M₃ to avoid paying f* twice.

So the picture is: the cooperative outcome is strictly better for both intermediaries, but it isn't a Nash equilibrium of the stage game — each has a unilateral incentive to deviate by entering the other's natural market and grabbing a share of A minus the entry cost f (a profitable deviation by assumption since A/2 ≥ f, so even half the natural market exceeds f). In a one-shot world, the model predicts wasteful Nash entry in every market. The next chapter shows how an infinite horizon and trigger strategies change the picture.
