CHAPTER 3Dividend play

A virtually riskless options-market arbitrage that arises every time a dividend-paying stock approaches its ex-dividend date. The laboratory for testing the BPS model.

§3.1 The mechanism

Consider an in-the-money American call option on a dividend-paying stock, approaching the last cum-dividend date. Standard option pricing (recall your derivatives course) says that early exercise is optimal for the holder when the dividend the underlying is about to pay exceeds the time value the option will lose by being exercised. In symbols, if D is the upcoming dividend, K is the strike, and r is the interest rate, the early-exercise condition is roughly D > Kr·Δt + (time value) (Hull's textbook, Chapter 10).

In practice, a non-trivial fraction of retail investors fail to exercise even when it is optimal — through inattention, computational difficulty, or operational frictions (Poteshman & Serbin 2003; Battalio, Figlewski & Neal 2020). The Options Clearing Corporation (OCC) processes the exercise notices it receives and randomly assigns these exercises to short positions. Short positions not assigned to deliver the stock retain the option and receive a windfall capital gain — they hold a contract that just became more valuable because the underlying didn't go ex-dividend on them.

The arbitrage opportunity is now visible. If you are a sophisticated trader, you can dilute the pool of short positions on the relevant contract just before the assignment is performed. You short a large number of additional contracts. Because OCC assignment is random across short positions, your incremental shorts shift the assignment probability toward you and away from the original short holder. By design, you exercise all of your own offsetting long positions, so you're left with only the unassigned short. With probability ≈ 1, you capture most of the unexercised contracts' windfall.

To stay perfectly hedged through the trade, you simultaneously buy the same number of identical contracts (or contracts with neighbouring strikes) from a pre-arranged counterparty. The long and short cancel for risk purposes; only the windfall capital gain remains. The counterparty does the mirror trade with you. Both of you exit with the windfall split between you, plus the original short holder gets crowded out.

Two structural features make this strategy clean for empirical work. First, the arbitrage is virtually riskless — the only residual exposure is the overnight risk of assignment timing, which is small. Second, the trade has a signature: a coordinated burst of buy-and-sell of large contract quantities, executed on a physical exchange floor (where the fees are capped), in seconds, on the last cum-dividend date. That signature is what makes the dataset usable.

§3.3 Why this is the right laboratory

Most of the limits-to-arbitrage literature struggles with two empirical obstacles: (1) you observe prices but not quantities of arbitrage capital, and (2) the "arbitrage opportunity" is often endogenous to the very prices you study. Dividend play sidesteps both:

  • Quantities are visible. Transaction-level OPRA data (with floor-trade flags) lets the authors directly observe each arbitrageur's entry into each contract, including the size of their committed positions.
  • Arbitrage value is exogenous. The windfall D·(contracts unexercised) doesn't depend on the arbitrageur's price impact — it depends on the dividend and on retail-investor failure to exercise, both fixed before the dividend play starts. The model's assumption that "A is exogenous and shared pro-rata by entrants" maps directly to reality here.
  • Few participants, repeated interaction. Most dividend-play volume goes through PHLX and BOX (the two exchanges that cap fees for this strategy). Floor trading is concentrated: small handful of arbitrageurs, all observing one another's trades over years of dividend-play calendar events. Exactly the environment the model is built for.

§3.4 Identifying arbitrageur trades

The data source is OPRA LiveVol (provided by Cboe), covering November 2019 through June 2023. The key innovation is the use of OPRA's detailed trade-type flags — specifically SLFT and MLFT (single-leg and multi-leg floor trades) — to isolate transactions executed from the physical exchange floor.

To estimate the number of arbitrageurs in a given contract, the authors exploit the fact that each dividend-play trade appears in OPRA as a tight burst of buy-then-sell trades within seconds, with one unique trade size per arbitrageur pair. The number of unique trade sizes within a burst proxies for the number of arbitrageur pairs participating. A pair counts as one "arbitrageur" because only one party in the pair receives the windfall — the other is just the counterparty providing the hedge.

With this in hand, the authors can now ask the model's questions directly. Of all profitable dividend-play contracts, what fraction attract any entry? Conditional on entry, how many arbitrageurs participate? Does the number of entrants vary with the size of the arbitrage opportunity? Those are the headline findings of Chapter 4.
