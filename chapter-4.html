CHAPTER 4What the data shows

Three empirical facts. Each is hard to square with competitive limits-to-arbitrage but lines up neatly with the strategic model's testable predictions.

§4.1 Three empirical facts

Aggregating across all profitable dividend-play opportunities in the November 2019 – June 2023 sample, the paper's central findings (§4.2 of the paper) reduce to three numbers.

Two things make these numbers striking. First, dividend play is not a marginal opportunity: the daily floor volume on cum-dividend dates exceeds the average daily volume by a factor of 100 on relevant tickers. Arbitrageurs are aware of the strategy and do trade it. The 43% non-entry isn't ignorance.

Second, the dollar values are large. $204M of forgone profit over ~3.5 years is real money for a "virtually riskless" arbitrage. For comparison, de Silva, Smith and So (2022) document a $360M wealth transfer from retail to market makers via earnings-related options trading over a seven-times-longer sample period.

§4.2 No pecking order

A natural first guess: maybe arbitrageurs prioritise the most profitable opportunities and just ignore marginal ones. This is the "pecking order" hypothesis implied by standard limits-to-arbitrage stories — capital is scarce, so deploy it to the most profitable trades first.

BPS show the pecking-order hypothesis fails. Sort contracts into terciles by total potential profit, by EEV (early exercise value), or by moneyness. The "no-entry" share is roughly the same across terciles:

The paper also performs a propensity-score-matched test (Appendix B.7): they match each entered contract to a non-entered contract on the full set of profitability characteristics. They show that contracts with both zero and positive floor volume exist across the entire propensity-score spectrum — so the "marginal-cost-of-trade" story has no statistical home to live in.

§4.3 Identifying natural markets in the data

The model's mechanism is that 43% no-entry is concentrated in contracts that are natural to some incumbent market maker — typically because she's already short the contract from her ordinary market-making activity, so capturing the windfall is costless for her and other arbitrageurs rationally stay away.

Testing this requires a proxy for "natural to a market maker." BPS use two complementary proxies, both built from the Nasdaq PHOTO order-flow data that classifies trades by counterparty type:

  • Retail-buy imbalances. When retail customers are net buyers of a particular call contract, market makers (the other side of those trades) accumulate short inventory in that contract. If retail then fails to exercise, market makers are the natural beneficiaries of the windfall.
  • Market-maker sell imbalances. Direct measurement of market-maker net sell flow in a contract.

Importantly, the result survives restricting the sample to the top profitability tercile. So we're not just observing that arbitrageurs avoid small opportunities — they specifically avoid profitable opportunities natural to someone else. That's precisely the model's mechanism.

§4.4 Ruling out alternatives

Any careful paper has to dispatch the alternative stories. BPS systematically rule out the main candidates:

Trading costs

  • Bid-ask spreads similar across entered and non-entered contracts
  • Exchange fees capped on PHLX/BOX — no incremental fee for additional contracts
  • Most dividend-play trades execute at the spread midpoint

Capital constraints

  • Long and short positions on the same ticker net to zero for capital purposes
  • Daily fee cap structure does not bind for most participants
  • Pecking-order hypothesis explicitly rejected (FIG 4.2)

Operational / overnight risk

  • Risk-related contract characteristics (delta, vega) similar across entered and non-entered
  • Case-study contracts in the paper have near-identical Greeks

SEC stigma / reputational

  • Same arbitrageurs enter other dividend-play contracts; no consistent stigma pattern
  • Does not explain why entry rates correlate with natural-market proxies

With these alternatives ruled out, the strategic model's mechanism — tacit collusion in segmented markets, with incumbents protecting their natural markets and entrants alternating in contested ones — emerges as the parsimonious explanation that fits all three facts simultaneously.

For the empirical-finance reader

Notice the paper's identification logic. It doesn't run a single headline regression. It builds three independent stylised facts that each fall out of the data in a transparent way, then it cross-validates the mechanism by using two pre-existing natural-market proxies from microstructure literature. The "no pecking order" finding is the linchpin — it's the result that breaks the leading alternative theory cleanly, with no parameter to tune.
