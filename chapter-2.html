CHAPTER 2Tacit collusion

The repeated game enlarges the set of subgame perfect equilibria. The Pareto efficient allocation, infeasible as a stage Nash, returns as an SPE supported by trigger strategies — provided the discount factor is high enough.

§2.1Why the repeated game

The Folk Theorem of repeated games (Fudenberg & Maskin 1986) says that for a sufficiently patient discount factor, any feasible and individually rational payoff vector of the stage game can be sustained as a subgame perfect equilibrium of the infinitely repeated game. Cooperation becomes self-enforcing because deviation today is punished by reversion to non-cooperative play tomorrow — and that punishment is credible.

In the BPS setting, the "cooperative" outcome we want to sustain is the symmetric Pareto-efficient allocation from Chapter 1: each intermediary stays in her own natural market, and they alternate entry in M₃. Both intermediaries are strictly better off than in the one-shot Nash. The only question is: can this cooperative pattern be sustained without explicit communication or contractual agreement?

This is what "tacit" in tacit collusion means. No one signs a deal. No one even talks. The collusive pattern emerges because each intermediary correctly anticipates that deviating today triggers a long-lasting punishment phase that destroys her future profits — and the future profits being protected are large enough that the short-term deviation gain isn't worth it.

Mapping to your IO coursework

Classical tacit collusion (Bernheim-Whinston 1990, Friedman 1971) is about setting prices above marginal cost in a Bertrand or Cournot setting. The novel feature in BPS is that cooperation is about not entering a market — the welfare gain comes from saving the fixed entry cost rather than from raising prices. Dutta-Madhavan (1997) is the closest predecessor, applying tacit collusion to NASDAQ market makers' odd-eighth quote-avoidance (Christie-Schultz 1994). BPS extend the logic to multi-market arbitrage.

§2.2The trigger strategy

BPS focus on a particular simple trigger strategy. Each intermediary maintains a binary mental state: either we are in the cooperative phase, or we are in the punishment phase. Play in the current period depends on this state and on observable history.

Critically, the trigger strategy requires only that intermediaries can observe whether someone deviated in the previous period. They don't need to communicate, agree, or sign anything. In the empirical application (Chapter 3), the public observability is provided by the OPRA transaction-level data — every dividend-play trade leaves a transparent flag that any participant can read.

§2.3The SPE condition

For the trigger strategy in FIG 2.1 to be a subgame perfect equilibrium, both intermediaries must prefer to keep cooperating over one-shot deviation, anticipating the punishment that follows. The one-shot deviation incentive constraint is:

Deviation gain today  ≤  Discounted loss tomorrow forever

Concretely: the most profitable deviation from the cooperative phase is to enter the opponent's natural market and capture the entire A there. Net of the entry cost f and the loss of one's share of M₃ in that period (which is being alternated), the immediate gain over the cooperative payoff works out to A − f. (BPS work this out carefully in the appendix; this is the cleanest characterisation.) The future cost is the per-period loss πPE − πNE = f + f*/2 from period t+1 onward, discounted by δ/(1−δ).

The cooperative path is sustainable as an SPE if and only if:

δ  ≥  (A − f − f*/2) / A EQ 2.1 · SPE CONDITION (PROPOSITION 3)

Notice the comparative statics. The required discount factor is lower — i.e. cooperation is easier to sustain — when the per-period gain from cooperation (f + f*/2) is larger relative to the arbitrage size A. When fixed costs are a big share of arbitrage profits, intermediaries have a lot to lose by triggering punishment, so they refrain from deviating even at relatively low discount factors.

§2.4Three testable predictions

Before turning to the data, the paper distils three sharp predictions of the tacit-collusion equilibrium (and one prediction of the contrary one-shot Nash hypothesis).

Tacit collusion (BPS hypothesis)

  • TP1. Natural markets are fully exploited by the incumbent natural arbitrageur. We should observe no entry by competitors.
  • TP2. In markets natural to neither player, we should observe only one arbitrageur entering.

One-shot Nash (null)

  • TP3. Arbitrageurs enter all markets. Resource commitments are proportional to arbitrage value: bigger A or A* attracts more capital.

The strategic-arbitrage model and the standard competitive limits-to-arbitrage model produce different qualitative predictions about which markets attract entry, not just about prices or quantities. That's the empirical opportunity. The next two chapters describe the laboratory.

A subtle point about the M₃ + M₄ extension

For the empirical comparison, BPS extend the model from one contested market M₃ to two equally-contested markets M₃ and M₄, each with the same arbitrage value and entry cost. In this extension, the symmetric tacit-collusion equilibrium has different intermediaries entering different contested markets — Intermediary 1 in M₃, Intermediary 2 in M₄ — with no need for period-by-period alternation. This makes the prediction "one arbitrageur per non-natural market" stationary and cleanly testable in cross-section, which is what the empirical section needs.
