01 · Overview

What this is

Bryzgalova, Pavlova and Sikorskaya (BPS) build a repeated game-theoretic model in which arbitrageurs face fixed entry costs and have natural markets — markets where the entry cost is zero, because the arbitrageur is already a market maker there or holds the right inventory. In a one-shot Nash equilibrium, both arbitrageurs enter all markets and equally split each opportunity. In an infinitely repeated game, however, the unique symmetric subgame perfect tacit-collusion equilibrium is much cleaner: each arbitrageur stays in her own natural market and the contested market alternates.

They test the model in the U.S. options "dividend play" market — a virtually riskless arbitrage that captures the windfall gains accruing to call-option writers when buyers fail to exercise before ex-dividend. Three findings, all consistent with the model and inconsistent with standard competitive limits-to-arbitrage theories: only 57% of profitable opportunities attract entry, arbitrageurs harvest only 44% of total potential profits, and 49% of contracts that do attract entry have just one arbitrageur. The final section shows how arbitrageurs circumvented the 2014 SEC clearing rule designed to kill this strategy.

For the year-1 PhD reader

The paper sits at the intersection of three literatures you saw in coursework: limits to arbitrage (Shleifer-Vishny 1997, Gromb-Vayanos 2002), repeated-game industrial organization (Green-Porter 1984, Bernheim-Whinston 1990, Dutta-Madhavan 1997), and options-market microstructure. The mechanism is novel relative to classical tacit-collusion IO models: cooperation here is about not entering a competitor's market, so the welfare gain comes from saving fixed costs rather than from raising prices.
